Origami-inspired morphing • Compact → Deployable

A hybrid UAV wing that folds small for tight spaces, then deploys for efficient flight.

Conventional fixed wings need clearance. We explored engineering-origami fold patterns to enable synchronized wing folding/unfolding with predictable deployed stiffness—aimed at safer operation in cluttered environments.

Project at a glance
Objective

Improve maneuverability and reduce crash risk by enabling in-air wing folding in confined environments.

Approach

Use engineering-origami kinematics for synchronized folding + predictable stiffness when deployed.

Outcome

Fold/unfold simulations + physical prototyping to evaluate compactness, feasibility, and integration.

The story

Make the wing disappear when you need clearance—bring it back when you need lift.

Rather than “more actuators,” the premise is geometry: folds that collapse the footprint for navigation and deploy to a stable planform for cruise.

01

Confined environments

Urban canyons, forests, and disaster zones demand compact flight configurations to avoid collisions and improve survivability.

02

Origami kinematics

Fold patterns synchronize motion across the span—turning many hinges into a predictable, single “mode” fold.

03

Deploy + lock

In deployed state, the wing must resist aerodynamic loads. A good pattern can support passive locking and stiffening.

Design

Patterns, mechanisms, and what we built.

We explored fold families (Miura-ori, Waterbomb, Kresling) and focused on patterns that can be prototyped with rigid panels and hinges.

Design concept

Two things matter most: predictable motion + deployed stiffness.

Instead of showing everything at once, hover these feature cards to reveal details.

Fold synchronization

Choose crease/hinge geometry that forces a consistent fold sequence rather than independent panel flutter.

Compact stack

Collapsed configuration should be clean and packable for transport + navigation near obstacles.

Minimal actuation

Bias toward passive or low-power mechanisms—geometry does most of the work.

Locking in deployment

In-flight configuration needs a stable “stop” so aerodynamic loads don’t re-fold the wing.
